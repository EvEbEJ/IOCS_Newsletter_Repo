    The Tegu

    Reptiles live everywhere. One extrodinary reptile is featured HERE. Check it out!

    Many reptiles can be found in North America and South America. The Tegu, (Salvator merianae) is a large lizard that grows up to more than 4 feet (about 1.2 meters)! As juveniles, these exotic reptiles are often kept as pets. But what happens once they grow to 4 and a half feet? Sometimes, owners don’t know better than to release them into the wild. Due to their having no predator and their quick reproduction, this reptile has invaded the Everglades. To make matters worse, tegus seize any opportunity to unearth and snack on the eggs of many creatures, which limits their population. Unchallenged, these reptiles are posing a great threat to the Everglade wildlife. Thankfully, conservation efforts are taking place to limit the harm that they are evoking. This is a perfect example of how the carelessness of man can harm creation. We should be good stewards of God’s creation, exercising proper dominion over what has been entrusted to us.

    The Chameleon’s Complex Chroma

    What is a chameleon? What is a chroma? How could these relate to each other? Find out NOW!

    Although many animals can change their colors, the chameleon stands alone as the animal with the widest range of colors and most unique system of metamorphosis. Other color-changing animals, such as the Mimic Octopus and Pacific Tree Frog, change the hue of their skin with the aid of pigments in their cells. The chameleon has a more technical process. This shade-shifting reptile has two thick skin layers constructed of iridophore cells which essentially work as mirrors. Iridophore cells contain nanocrystals differing in size and orientation. When a chameleon is relaxed, the nanocrystals (and iridophore cells) are close together. This produces colors with shorter wavelengths, such as blue. Conversely, when a chameleon is excited, the nanocrystals are stretched out within the skin and shorter wavelengths are produced. This creates lighter colors, including red, orange, and yellow. There is also a second, thicker layer of skin under the first. This iridophore layer continues infrared-absorbing nanocrystals. The chameleon uses this second layer to regulate body temperature. The chameleon’s skin is by far the most technically advanced out of all color-changing creatures.

    There are so many different types of reptiles. There are long ones, like the reticulated python, reaching a length of over 6.25m; there are reptiles that have a hard shell on their back and there is an interestingly-colored reptile known as the blue-tailed skink (Cryptoblepharus egeriae). This small skink can grow to the length of 4-5cm from its head to its vent. The blue-tailed skink has a black-brown striped body, but the most striking feature of this vibrant coloured creature is, unsurprisingly, its radiant bright blue tail.

    Interestingly, when the blue-tailed skink is threatened it drops off its brightly coloured tail and this is meant to distract the predator so the skink can quickly escape from the danger of being eaten. This species inhabits the glowing green Christmas Island of Australia. They were found all over the island until introduced predators wiped out the entirety of blue-tailed skinks in the wild.

    Mission


    from the IOCS Directors

    It is always important to know what you are doing. It is also important to let others know what you are doing. IOCS has been doing a lot, for example, in our newsletter we feature photos, articles, and more. But is that the IOCS' goal? To take photos or to defeat evolution?

    The answer to those questions is a definite no. We are not here to show what we can do, what we can accomplish. For if we were, we could not and would not survive very long. And even if we did, our photos and articles will have no power. They would be empty.

    But our primary focus is to glorify God in all that we do. For we can do anything with God’s strength (Philippians 4:13). God is the one on whom we are looking to all times.

    This is a reminder and reaffirmation both to IOCS members and to our readers. We are not here to elevate ourselves or our organization, but to reach the unreachable (Matthew 28:19-20), and to do the impossible (Luke 1:37) to God’s glory.
