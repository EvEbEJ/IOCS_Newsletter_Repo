    Orchids

    Flowers - they're all unique in their own ways. Find out about the crazy orchid flowers HERE!

    There are 28,000 species in the Orchidaceae family, making it the largest family of Herbaceous plants. Herbaceous is a word that here means “without a woody stem”. Although Orchids are famous for their specialized species that vary widely from one another, all Orchids have a few things in common. Most orchids have simple leaves with veins that run parallel to each other. Orchids have two types of roots: Monopodial, wherein the entire plant stems from a single bud, and Sympodial, which grows laterally with shoots adjacent to each other.

    However, orchids are most known for how they differ from one another. Each species of orchid has its own unique way of pollination, flower structure, and leaf structure. Orchids range from all sizes from the 3-feet tall Christmas Star Orchid in Madagascar to the Platystele Ovatilabia in Mexico which measures one-tenth of an inch.

    Macro vs. Micro. Evolution vs. Science. Read THIS article for a comprehensive explanation of key points in the creation-evolution world.

    Often, Evolution is referred to as science. It is referred to as observable. Let’s take a closer look at the credibility of this statement. Is Evolution observable?

    Before we begin, let’s define something really briefly: the differences between microevolution and macroevolution. The unnoticeable switch between the “i” and the “a” in “microevolution” and “macroevolution” is indispensable.

    Macroevolution is the evolution of one kind to another kind. This form of evolution is very drastic and is said to occur over millions of years. Examples of this phenomenon are fish (such as coelacanths) evolving into amphibians, dinosaurs evolving into birds ( see why is this not true ), and humans evolving from apes (see why this is not reliable).

    What’s the Deal With Carbon Dating?

    Is the 'the fundamental premise of carbon-14 dating' flawed? Find out HERE!


    If you have been involved in Evolution discourse at any point in time, you have probably heard the phrase ‘Carbon Dating’ thrown around a couple times. This article will tackle what Carbon Dating is, its reliability, and why it is so important to evolution discussions.

    Carbon dating is a method scientists use to predict the age of a dead organism. Here is how it works: When an organism is alive, it consumes carbon. Most of this carbon is carbon-12, which has a stable amount of neutrons. However, some carbon is an unstable isotope of carbon made from nitrogen-14 in the upper atmosphere. This isotope is called Carbon-14. When an organism dies, it stops consuming carbon, and carbon-14 decays back into nitrogen-14 by emitting beta particles. Scientists measure the number of beta particles emitted and calculate, using the assumed rate of carbon-14’s decay, the age of the organisms.

    Mission


    from the IOCS Directors

    It is always important to know what you are doing. It is also important to let others know what you are doing. IOCS has been doing a lot, for example, in our newsletter we feature photos, articles, and more. But is that the IOCS' goal? To take photos or to defeat evolution?

    The answer to those questions is a definite no. We are not here to show what we can do, what we can accomplish. For if we were, we could not and would not survive very long. And even if we did, our photos and articles will have no power. They would be empty.

    But our primary focus is to glorify God in all that we do. For we can do anything with God’s strength (Philippians 4:13). God is the one on whom we are looking to all times.

    This is a reminder and reaffirmation both to IOCS members and to our readers. We are not here to elevate ourselves or our organization, but to reach the unreachable (Matthew 28:19-20), and to do the impossible (Luke 1:37) to God’s glory.
