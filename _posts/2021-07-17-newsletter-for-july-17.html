    Back to the Basics | Worldview Lenses

    by Evan Jiang

    A worldview is anything that someone uses to form an opinion or to interpret a fact. For example, would you rather own a cat or a dog? Your worldview, or bias, would affect your decision remarkably.

    Here’s an actual creation-evolution example:

    A leading creationist geologist wrote a paper on a discovery in Australia1, and an evolutionary website wrote a critique2.

    The creationist put forth these facts:

    1. A fossilized slab of wood had a radiocarbon date which did not match the strata3 in which it was discovered.
    2. According to the radiocarbon, the fossil was “only” a few thousand years old, while the rock from which the fossil was excavated was several million years old.

    The evolutionists pointed out the following:

    1. The fossil may not have retained its original carbon.
    2. The fossil may have been contaminated.

    The evolutionists winnowed out many possibilities with their worldview lens.4 First, since the fossil was supposed to be millions of years old (according to the strata), it would not have had any original carbon. They started with the premise that the fossil was millions of years old. However, what if the evolutionary geologic column is inaccurate? Next, they attempted to excuse the date by giving the all-too-often explanation that the wood had been contaminated. I wonder why so many radiocarbon dates are contaminated. Could it be that radiocarbon dates (which, in reality, are estimates) are unreliable?

    You see, everyone interprets evidence through their own set of worldview lenses.

    Bivalves

    Evidence All Along1

    by Ebenezer Jiang

    Bivalves are mollusks that have two shells that guard the owner’s soft bodies. If you’ve ever been to the beach, you might have seen, found, or caught a bivalve, such as a clam or oyster, before. Throughout the world, these shellfish are very common and well known. So, it’s not surprising that there are over 15,000 living species of clams, and 500 live in freshwater.2 But clams are not only found as shellfish in sand. Not at all! They are also found as fossils in rock! Many specimens of clam fossils have been gathered throughout the world. Say, how did these fossils form? Let’s find out.

    Many people think that evolution is science, and believe in it. Really? Trying to exclude God from every part of life, Evolutionists put clam fossils under the category of millions and even billions of years. And, like for all fossils, slow and constant burial does the trick.

    Really? Is that scientific? No. Clams are bivalves, meaning that they have two shells, which are connected by a very strong muscle. When danger is sensed or if they feel threatened, clams will tightly close their shells together. After death, this muscle relaxes, and the two halves of the shell open up; this is why at the beach, it is easier to find one half of a clam than to find two complete clam shells that stayed together. Annoyingly, this factor acts as a thorn in the foot of the theory of Evolution, because if a clam lays in one place for thousands or maybe millions of years, waiting to get buried, it will undoubtedly be dead. But if this is the case, why are there clam fossils with tightly closed shells? This could only mean a quick and catastrophic burial. (Sounds like a flood?)

    It is evident and explicit. Because Evolution removes God, the evolutionary theory of millions of years proves to be no more than a fable.

    Creation gives a broad, straight, and errorless response. Like all marine creatures, clams were fashioned by God on the third day (a literal 24-hour day) of creation. Contrary to Evolution, Creationists believe that the Flood, sent by God, buried all of life on earth, except for that on the ark. This would have rapidly and catastrophically buried clams globally. This would also explain their tense, closed position. Doubtless, they were frightened by the great, pounding waves that the flood displayed. Also, the clams would have been buried alive and would have had no chance of escaping the tons of sediment poured on them.

    Even clams can show us God’s intricate design and splendor and the flawlessness of his word. Indeed, Agur, that is, the writer of Proverbs 30, was right when he said, “Every word of God is pure: he is a shield unto them that put their trust in him.”3 We can put our trust in God not only because of all the evidence in fossils and other forms that he has shown to us, but also for his errorless, trustworthy word, which he has given to us. To him be glory and honor forever and ever.

    The Woolly Mammoth

    by Christian Conti

    Above: A mammoth according to an artist's interpretation.

    Woolly Mammoths (Mammuthus primigenius) are large hairy elephant-like creatures that lived hundreds of thousands of years ago. Wrong! Mammoths surely did not live this far back. How do we know this? As it turns out, many mammoths that fell into glaciers or ravines have been found by paleontologists nearly perfectly preserved! No animal could ever have stayed preserved for that long of a time.

    So what exactly is a Woolly Mammoth? They are incredibly similar to modern-day elephants, sharing many characteristics. However, instead of living in arid, hot places, they lived in the icy cold tundras of Siberia. Mammoths are covered in long, thick fur that protects them against subzero temperatures. They are about the size of an Indian Elephant and have a similar appearance. However unlike the Indian Elephant, mammoths have gigantic tusks that can be nearly 14 feet (4.2 meters) and weigh over 200 pounds (90.7 kg)!

    The Walkingstick

    by Evan Jiang

    Above: A Northern Walkingstick blends in with the pine needles. Photo by Evan Jiang.

    What animal has the best camouflage? The Walkingstick, or stick insect, is arguably near the top. Walkingsticks (order Phasmatodea)1 are slow insects that quite notably resemble sticks. Walkingsticks can reach enormous lengths, with the longest of those collected reaching 62.4 centimeters (~2 feet)! This makes them the longest insects on record!

    The walkingstick’s impressive camouflage is key to its survival. Every part of this insect resembles a stick: it displays lanky, twig-like legs, a neutral color, and of course, its slender body. However, walkingsticks have more tricks up the sleeve. For starters, they will sway in the wind, making it all the more difficult to find them. Even if they are discovered, walkingsticks have a variety of backup defenses. These insects will shed their legs in order to distract their predators. Some species will even release an odious smell or a liquid that blinds their enemies.2
