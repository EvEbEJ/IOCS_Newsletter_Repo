    The Ice Age

    by Evan Jiang

    There is much evidence for an ice age (e.g. formations formed by ‘extinct’ glaciers). But when most people think of an ice age, they imagine that it is an evolutionary concept. However, many creationists also believe that it occurred!

    Many differences are present between creationist and evolutionist views on the ice age. While evolutionists assume that several ice ages have occurred, creationists believe that only one took place. Also, evolutionists do not have a satisfactory answer to how an ice age is formed.1

    In contrast, creationists have an extraordinary event that would have triggered an ice age: the Flood. During the Flood, lots of volcanic activity would have developed, and with those volcanoes would come massive quantities of ash. Ash would block the sun from reaching the earth, thus causing a cooler climate. We have seen this event happen in much smaller proportions (e.g. A.D. 1816 “the Year without a Summer”). Additionally, the volcanic activity and warm water gushing out from the “fountains of the deep” would have significantly increased ocean water temperatures. Warmer ocean water would evaporate rather quickly, and provide the cloud masses necessary for all the snow during the ice age.2

    To conclude this article, I will provide the two main points.

    1. Evolutionists cannot explain ice ages according to their model.
    2. The ice age fits like a missing puzzle piece in the creationist model.

    The "Exploring the Fossils” column will contain a monthly article about fossils. August's fossil:


    Trilobites

    Tripling Bites On Evolution1

    by Ebenezer Jiang

    Trilobite. When many people hear this word, they immediately shrug it off as some random extinct organism, but the trilobite has more to offer than just an extinct critter. The word “trilobite” means “three lobes.” If you say it slowly, notice how much it sounds like “tri-lobe-ite.” (“Tri-” for triple (or three), “lobe” for lobe, and bites, because then it sounds like an organism.)2 Most, if not everyone, who know about trilobites, confirm that they are extinct. (You don’t just go to the beach and find them anymore.) Well, how did they become extinct? This is where Evolution and Creation begin to battle. Which is right? Which is wrong?

    Evolution insists that these exotic crustaceans dwelt on the earth in the Paleozoic era, that is 250 to 570 million years ago. How were they fossilized? ‘Simple!’ Evolutionists would state, ‘Over a few million or tens of thousands of years, and over slow, constant burial. Eventually, the trilobite would be totally submerged in an air-proof matter and fossilization would occur.’ See a problem with these monolithic assumptions?

    Trilobites are like roly-polies (check out our roly-poly article HERE!); they have the amazing ability to curl up into a tight ball when frightened. And we have observed many fossils of these marine creatures in such compacted and curled up poses. Now, when trilobites die, they uncurl because, similar to a clam, the muscle that holds them in a ball relaxes after death. Okay, so why do we see trilobites rolled up? If this were the case, they would have to be alive. Even if Evolution says that a few “lucky ones” got buried when in this pose, this argument is extremely invalid because we don’t just observe a few, but many such specimens of curled-up trilobites!! What is the chance of that happening? Another obstacle is tissue. How can we find intact tissue in trilobite fossils that lived over 100 million years ago and were buried in a way that would kill the tissue? There is no reasonable explanation for how such tissue could remain for such a bizarre duration of time. And this is only the surface. Many factors about the trilobite bite into Evolutionary ideas. Obviously, Evolution doesn’t seem to be very secure. Perhaps there is a better solution to the “trilobotic” dilemma.

    Creationism stands with a probability that is above and beyond the zenith of the probability of Evolution’s explanation. Creationism teaches that the world is about 6000 to 7000 years old. If this is the case, trilobites would most certainly not be millions of years old. The Flood probably occurred 4000 to 5000 years ago. Now, the Flood would have quickly buried life. The catastrophism, the earth’s tectonic movement, and all of the movement and water would have instantaneously frozen creatures in place. Now, this could be the case with trilobites. The crustaceans, frightened from the flood’s presence, would instinctively roll up into a ball. This would explain why we find fossils of curled-up trilobites since the trilobites were buried alive and would not be uncurled. Also, the tissue would only have to last at most 5000 years. It is obvious, there is no more need for millions and gazillions of years.

    In Job 36:26, Elihu (Job’s friend) says, “Behold, God is great, and we know him not, neither can the number of his years be searched out.” Indeed, how true these words are! God is so great. Even the itty-bitty trilobites, which He fashioned, points to His great workmanship. Isn’t it amazing that our God can do what Evolution would need billions of years for, in less than a few thousand years? (And even less if he wanted to!) How great is our God! Let us magnify His name with the words from Psalm 8:1, which says, “O Lord, our Lord, how excellent is thy name in all the earth! who hast set thy glory above the heavens.”

    The column, "Dinosaur of the Week," is a weekly article. This week's article:

    Purussaurus

    by Christian Conti

    Above: An artist's interpretation of the Purussaurus.

    Although the name Purussaurus (named after the Purus river) sounds much like a dinosaur name, it was not a dinosaur but actually a species of large crocodilian —— so large in fact it dwarfed even the largest of crocodilians living today. It is a contender for the most gigantic crocodilian ever. Growing over 40 feet in length, few creatures were quite this size, not even Tyrannosaurus! Two other crocodilians that could have been as big if not bigger are Sarcosuchus and Deinosuchus.

    Surprisingly, Purussaurus is not a true crocodile or an alligator. It is actually a species of caiman. This giant caiman would have likely eaten any creature in its ecosystem; an unparalleled apex predator. Purussaurus also had one of the most powerful bites in the animal kingdom at over 12 tons. To put that into perspective, it is like having two elephants or four pick up trucks on top of you, or 20 times the force of a great white shark’s bite!

    The Redback Spider

    by Ebenezer Jiang

    Above: One of the deadliest spiders on the planet - the Redback. Photo by Earnest Law.

    Most people are frightened by bugs. Centipedes, worms, and mosquitoes to name a few. But perhaps the one bug that most people are scared of is the spider. One specific spider, the Redback spider (Latrodectus hasselti) is most unique. Redbacks live mostly in Oceania (New Zealand, Australia), but they are also found in southeast Asia, and even in Belgium, the United Arab Emirates, Iran, Japan, and England!1

    WARNING: Redbacks are venomous! If you are bitten by a Redback Spider, seek medical attention immediately.


    Redbacks are one of the most venomous spiders on the planet. Redbacks resemble Black Widows of the United States, with a reddish to orangish hourglass on the underside of their abdomens. But redbacks also have an addition. On the top of their black or brown abdomens, female redbacks have a bold red streak that runs down their back. This appearance gives the redback a hideous appearance, which also warns potential attackers, "Look out!" Of all the compounds found in their venom, only one compound (alpha-latrotoxin) can cause death in humans.2 Yet because of the Redback's jaw's size, only 10-20% of the bites on humans actually manage to inject venom.3,4 Redbacks use this venom not only for defense but also for hunting. Redbacks’ webs are constructed of a mass of threads with sticky, vertical silk threads latching onto the ground. When an insect gets trapped in this obstacle course, redbacks inject their lethal venom and then, the insect is doomed. Sometimes, redbacks even capture larger animals, like lizards and snakes.

    Even though the redback is only 1 centimeter (female; males are 3 to 4 millimeters) long, they are surely one of the spiders with Intelligent Design, not only do they have a way to catch prey, but they also have a defense that could be provided only by the Great Maker.
