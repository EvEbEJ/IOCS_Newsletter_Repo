    The Direction of Evolution

    by Evan Jiang

    Many people think of variation within a kind as microevolution and molecule-to-man evolution as macroevolution, but are these terms adequate? Do they explain the real difference between these two processes?

    The correct answer is no. The amount of variation or change which micro- and macro- imply is not the ultimate or most accurate dividing point between microevolution and macroevolution. A more precise division is the direction of evolution. You see, with evolution, genes are ever-increasing in an individual. A single-celled microorganism can transform into a multicellular, complex animal. The genes are increasing.

    However, creation presents a different story. In the beginning, God already set each animal within each kind. These first animals probably had a diverse assortment of genes. Their children would inherit some of this genetic diversity, but some would be lost. Pretty soon, you end up with painted turtle eggs hatching into painted turtles and chicken eggs hatching into chicks. “Microevolution” follows a downward trend.

    So microevolution and macroevolution are not necessarily divided by size, but by direction. Microevolution goes downhill (genetic diversity is decreasing); macroevolution goes uphill (genetic diversity is increasing).

    The "Exploring the Fossils” column will contain a monthly article about fossils. August's fossil:


    Trilobites

    Tripling Bites On Evolution1

    by Ebenezer Jiang

    Trilobite. When many people hear this word, they immediately shrug it off as some random extinct organism, but the trilobite has more to offer than just an extinct critter. The word “trilobite” means “three lobes.” If you say it slowly, notice how much it sounds like “tri-lobe-ite.” (“Tri-” for triple (or three), “lobe” for lobe, and bites, because then it sounds like an organism.)2 Most, if not everyone, who know about trilobites, confirm that they are extinct. (You don’t just go to the beach and find them anymore.) Well, how did they become extinct? This is where Evolution and Creation begin to battle. Which is right? Which is wrong?

    Evolution insists that these exotic crustaceans dwelt on the earth in the Paleozoic era, that is 250 to 570 million years ago. How were they fossilized? ‘Simple!’ Evolutionists would state, ‘Over a few million or tens of thousands of years, and over slow, constant burial. Eventually, the trilobite would be totally submerged in an air-proof matter and fossilization would occur.’ See a problem with these monolithic assumptions?

    Trilobites are like roly-polies (check out our roly-poly article HERE!); they have the amazing ability to curl up into a tight ball when frightened. And we have observed many fossils of these marine creatures in such compacted and curled up poses. Now, when trilobites die, they uncurl because, similar to a clam, the muscle that holds them in a ball relaxes after death. Okay, so why do we see trilobites rolled up? If this were the case, they would have to be alive. Even if Evolution says that a few “lucky ones” got buried when in this pose, this argument is extremely invalid because we don’t just observe a few, but many such specimens of curled-up trilobites!! What is the chance of that happening? Another obstacle is tissue. How can we find intact tissue in trilobite fossils that lived over 100 million years ago and were buried in a way that would kill the tissue? There is no reasonable explanation for how such tissue could remain for such a bizarre duration of time. And this is only the surface. Many factors about the trilobite bite into Evolutionary ideas. Obviously, Evolution doesn’t seem to be very secure. Perhaps there is a better solution to the “trilobotic” dilemma.

    Above: A trilobite of the genus Flexicalymene.

    Creationism stands with a probability that is above and beyond the zenith of the probability of Evolution’s explanation. Creationism teaches that the world is about 6000 to 7000 years old. If this is the case, trilobites would most certainly not be millions of years old. The Flood probably occurred 4000 to 5000 years ago. Now, the Flood would have quickly buried life. The catastrophism, the earth’s tectonic movement, and all of the movement and water would have instantaneously frozen creatures in place. Now, this could be the case with trilobites. The crustaceans, frightened from the flood’s presence, would instinctively roll up into a ball. This would explain why we find fossils of curled-up trilobites since the trilobites were buried alive and would not be uncurled. Also, the tissue would only have to last at most 5000 years. It is obvious, there is no more need for millions and gazillions of years.

    In Job 36:26, Elihu (Job’s friend) says, “Behold, God is great, and we know him not, neither can the number of his years be searched out.” Indeed, how true these words are! God is so great. Even the itty-bitty trilobites, which He fashioned, points to His great workmanship. Isn’t it amazing that our God can do what Evolution would need billions of years for, in less than a few thousand years? (And even less if he wanted to!) How great is our God! Let us magnify His name with the words from Psalm 8:1, which says, “O Lord, our Lord, how excellent is thy name in all the earth! who hast set thy glory above the heavens.”

    The column, "Dinosaur of the Week," is a weekly article. This week's article:

    Arthropleura

    by Christian Conti

    Above: An artist's interpretation of the Arthropleura.

    Of all the creepy critters that crawled on the earth, Arthropleura (rib joint), is definitely one of the creepiest. Why might you ask? Well, being a millipede, it is already pretty scary, but when you supersize it, it becomes a monster straight out of science fiction. This gargantuan arthropod grew to otherworldly sizes that one might consider unnatural. The largest specimens grew to nearly 8 and a half (2.6 meters) feet in length, making it the most massive terrestrial invertebrate ever to walk the earth!

    Although this creature might seem terrifying, it was harmless and probably only fed on ferns and decayed plants. It was a gentle giant. Because of its immense size, it probably did not have to worry about predators.

    The Stink Bug

    by Ebenezer Jiang

    Above: The notorious stink bug rests peacefully on a plant. Photo by Evan Jiang.

    Now, you may be looking at the title of this Creature of the Week and be saying to yourself, “Ebenezer, seriously?” Okay, I would admit that the Stink Bug (family Pentatomidae) has an unusual name and isn’t the subject of your everyday conversation, but it still is an insect, and not only that, it’s an insect with Intelligent Design. Stink bugs are “true bugs.” They have six legs and their wings form an “X” on their back. They are similar to an assassin bug (and the fascinating Ambush Bug), but are much shorter and stouter.

    WARNING: Although rare, stink bugs have the potential to bite. Their bite is often compared to a bee sting that lasts for a long time, so use caution.


    Alright, so what’s with the name of the Stink Bug? Yep! That’s right, stink bugs stink. When they are threatened, stink bugs produce a foul smelling chemical in their abdomen that is often strong enough to repel humans and other potential predators. Some species can even spray this odorous chemical several inches! This defense is compounded because the smell can last for up to several hours! But wait! Don’t go stepping on every stink bug you see! Stink bugs can give off this pungent odor if they are injured or crushed as well! Isn’t the stink bug so Intelligently Designed? Some predators will still go for creatures that have a pungent taste, ugly or frightening appearance, a loud, defensive noise, or a nasty bite. But with a repulsive smell, well, on second thought, let’s leave that insect alone.1

    God is so great in all his creations. Not only does he make each one able to function and operate, but he also cares for each one of them similar to the ways he cares for the stink bug. God cares for even the smallest of creatures, so we can be sure that he will care for us.

    The Mission of IOCS

    from the IOCS Directors

    It is always important to know what you are doing. It is also important to let others know what you are doing. IOCS has been doing a lot, for example, in our newsletter we feature photos, articles, and more. But is that the IOCS' goal? To take photos or to defeat evolution?

    The answer to those questions is a definite no. We are not here to show what we can do, what we can accomplish. For if we were, we could not and would not survive very long. And even if we did, our photos and articles will have no power. They would be empty.

    But our primary focus is to glorify God in all that we do. For we can do anything with God’s strength (Philippians 4:13). God is the one on whom we are looking to all times.

    This is a reminder and reaffirmation both to IOCS members and to our readers. We are not here to elevate ourselves or our organization, but to reach the unreachable (Matthew 28:19-20), and to do the impossible (Luke 1:37) to God’s glory.
